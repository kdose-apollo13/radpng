The Radical Zen of Python
=========================

One atom, one file.
One job, one function (when that job forms a natural, independently meaningful unit).
Name the exports at the top; say no more than needed.

Atoms before monoliths.
Composition before inheritance.
Orchestrate at the edges; keep the core dumb.

Explicit beats clever.
Plain loops beat magic.
Bytes beat objects; dicts beat classes—unless the GUI demands otherwise.

Read it top to bottom.
If you cannot, split it.
(If splitting increases the burden of understanding a semantic facility or makes equal-signature replacement harder, name that cost and stop.)

Cognitive burden is the enemy; simplicity is the weapon.

`: ` for what goes in.
`> ` for what comes out.
`! ` for what must not happen.
Say the contract; skip the sermon.

Raise ValueError; name the sin.
NotImplemented is honest refusal, not shame.
Tradeoffs admitted in the docstring are tradeoffs kept.

Pure stdlib only.
No deps, no drama.
What the language already gives you is enough.

Every module is a demo.
`if __name__ == '__main__'`: run the atom (or the small coherent facility), prove it.
The file (or tight cluster) teaches itself.

Thin library, thick tests.
Test-only helpers stay in tests.
Pack what callers must pack; do not smuggle convenience into the surface.

One test file per (major) atom or semantic facility.
Given, When, Then—every scenario speaks plain English.
Short aliases in tests; long names in libraries.

Symmetry proves correctness.
Encode then decode; filter then unfilter.
Roundtrip is the oracle.

Radical is not reckless.
Radical is Patton on complexity: decompose, march, accept the cost.
Some generality deferred is complexity slain.

A healthy codebase exhibits a power-law distribution of module sizes.
Most files are small and focused (true atoms or leaves).
A modest number of "unified semantic facilities" are larger — they group things that are usually understood, varied, or demoed together (row geometry, the filter transform, the scaling/reporting experience, the PNG encoding pipeline, etc.).

Bias strongly toward small when a clean single job exists.
But design explicitly for the power law that serves:
- semantic coherence (you can point to "the scaling facility" or "the PNG row transform" without opening a dozen files),
- low combinatorial explosion (reasonable imports when using a common story),
- easy understanding and demo at a useful abstraction level,
- swappability for the optimization phase (equal-signature pieces or small coherent groups live close together and are convenient to replace via variants, contexts, or direct substitution while keeping high-level scenarios locked).

When the rule feels ridiculous, try the split (or the consolidation) anyway.
The observation of the resulting density and its effect on correctness, comprehension, and future variation is part of the practice.

Beautiful tests, boring code.
Informative failures, quiet successes.
When all pass: Radical!

Observe what the rules create.
Duplication in 2nd-order tests may be the honest price of isolation.
Over-fragmentation raises the cost of variation and makes the flock harder to navigate.
Under-fragmentation hides seams that should be swappable.
Name both. Tune deliberately. Evolve the ethos as you learn what density actually serves the work.

This document is itself an atom (or small coherent set of principles).
When it no longer reads top to bottom or no longer helps you ship correct, understandable, easily varied code, rewrite it.
